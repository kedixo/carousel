Story Forms | January 2017

Oak

Oak is a reimagined story-creation tool that is fully integrated with a variety of media, and built to support an expressive range of storytelling.

When to use it: When you have a variety of contextual media, complex typography needs, and a narrative that lends itself to an interplay between text and media.

How to get started: At this stage in the rollout, we're asking users to use Oak only for articles that would otherwise be Listys. Have a look at our Oak training and support website for information on how to get started.

Photo Essay

Showcase our photo desk's exceptional work, intermingling images and text to provide a visually driven narrative with a strong emotional impact.

When to use it: Use a Photo Essay to tell visual stories through high-quality photography. The images are the main storytelling vehicle — text is used to provide context.

How to get started: If you’re working on a story that will have strong visuals, collaborate with your photo editor early on in the process.

By The Numbers

Break down complex news with big, surprising numbers.

When to use it: Use By The Numbers when there’s are remarkable numericals relevant to the story. Look for big numbers that, when threaded together in context, give structure and a distinctive look to a complex story.

How to get started: For guidance on using By The Numbers, contact the Digital Transition team at dt@nytimes.com.

Q&A Explainer

Advance stories and provide context by answering the questions on everybody’s mind.

When to use it: Think about a news story — what questions do you have about it? It’s likely readers have similar questions. Use a Q&A Explainer when the news creates more questions than answers.

How to get started: For guidance on using Q&A Explainers, contact the Digital Transition team at dt@nytimes.com.

Documents

Enables you to publish public documents with your articles — and to index hundreds of pages of information in seconds.

When to use it: Use Document Cloud in conjunction with Scoop Documents to publish any public document that relates directly to your reporting.

How to get started: Have a look at our Documents training and support website for information on how to get started.

Chatblog

Chatblog is a tool that enables us to build intimate connections with readers through live chats.

When to use it: For live news events that unfold on a moment-by-moment basis—for example, major sporting events—or for planned question-and-answer sessions.

How to get started: Have a look at our Chatblog training and support website for information on how to get started.

What We Know (And Don't)

A standard article that brings readers up to speed with a summary of what's known and not known about a single news topic.

When to use it: Use What We Know (And Don't) when a story has a lot of moving parts — breaking news or a slowly developing topic. It gives readers a clear sense of where the story stands and creates transparency around the facts.

How to get started: For guidance on using What We Know, contact the Digital Transition team at dt@nytimes.com.

Perspectives

Provides a narrative inside look into a news event through first-person accounts.

When to use it: Use Perspectives as an alternative to a tick-tock for big news stories. The first-person accounts tell the story through a mix of witness voices.

How to get started: For guidance on using Perspectives, contact the Digital Transition team at dt@nytimes.com.

Social Media Narrative

Tell a story through the use of contextually-embedded social media.

When to use it: Use Social Media Narratives when a story is showing a strong presence on Twitter or other social media platforms.

How to get started: For guidance on using Social Media Narratives, contact the Digital Transition team at dt@nytimes.com.

Subheads & Media

Uses subheads — enriched by contextual media like Tweets, videos and pull-quotes — to make an article more expressive.

When to use it: Use Subheads and Media when a story is built around digital elements such as Tweets and videos. Use subheads to break up a story.

How to get started: For guidance on using Subheads and Contextual Media, contact the Digital Transition team at dt@nytimes.com.

Live Briefing

Live Briefings are a flexible format designed for fast-moving events. They are meant to provide a comprehensive digest for readers.

When to use it: Use Live Briefings to provide perspective on major news events that have a large scope, multiple storylines, and regular updates. Faster than a standard Scoop article, slower than Chatblog.

How to get started: Have a look at our Live Briefing training and support website for information on how to get started.

Guide

An article that provides every practical functional detail a reader needs to know — times, dates, locations, events — about a specific news event.

When to use it: Use Guides when we have an opportunity to arm our readers with information that will prepare them to attend a specific news event, or monitor it in an infomed way.

How to get started: For guidance on using Guides, contact the Digital Transition team at dt@nytimes.com.
